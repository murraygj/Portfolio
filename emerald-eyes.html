Emerald Eyes

Building a premium fashion brand from doctrine to demand

Overview

An Integrated Brand System

Emerald Eyes is a premium fashion label I built end-to-end — identity, creative direction, Shopify commerce, and paid acquisition, all running as one connected system. Not a product launch, but a live build: create a brand world, put it in front of the market, and use real behavior data to refine both the strategy and the doctrine.

The brand generated strong pull before it had fully built the trust needed to convert at scale. That gap became the most useful finding of the entire project.

The Challenge

The Objective

Build a premium brand with enough clarity and conviction to generate real demand — while engineering the commerce and acquisition system well enough to see exactly where the customer journey held and where it broke.

Emerald Eyes was never meant to function like trend-led streetwear. The doctrine frames it as a standard, not a plea for attention: construct an environment, create resonance, let the right people recognize the signal.

My Role

End-to-End Ownership

I led the project end-to-end as founder, brand architect, creative director, Shopify operator, and performance strategist — from positioning and visual language through storefront build, Meta Pixel integration, segmented campaign structure, and funnel-stage creative iteration.

Brand System

Doctrine & Visual Language

Emerald Eyes is built on a conviction: vision demands to be lived out, not admired. The doctrine extends that into a second principle — the discipline of learning to see — which frames the brand less as self-expression and more as alignment through creation.

The public-facing posture is restraint, precision, and seriousness. Internally, the system is a laboratory — analytical, iterative, and conversion-aware. That split became one of the most useful frameworks in the project.

Brand doctrine sample, identity application, and visual language in practice.

The system could generate desire. The constraint was trust.

Offer Structure

Launch & Offer Evolution

The early rollout centered on premium hoodies at $170, paired with a free $90 T-shirt, free shipping, and urgency mechanics. During Black Friday, selected SKUs were marked down, then returned to premium pricing as the offer structure evolved.

The point: Emerald Eyes was run as a live market system. Offer framing, promotional mechanics, and funnel behavior were all variables under active testing — not fixed decisions.

Store & Funnel Architecture

Commerce & Funnel Infrastructure

The storefront was built as a brand environment first — high-contrast hero, direct CTA, collection visibility, mission language, and email/SMS capture — rather than a stripped-down product page.

The paid system was segmented across funnel stages: cold-audience attraction at the top, retargeting deeper in. Because purchase volume was still limited, optimization focused on intermediate signals — traffic and add-to-cart — rather than forcing premature purchase optimization.

Storefront design and segmented funnel structure

Creative Strategy

Creative & Campaign Assets

Different assets served different psychological jobs. Video performed best at the top of funnel — cinematic, mood-driven pieces that created atmosphere before explanation. Product stills performed best closer to conversion, translating visual pull into clicks and add-to-cart behavior once the audience had context. Video built gravity. Stills closed distance.

Video for atmosphere, stills for conversion

A cold-audience still-image ad for the Cross Hoodie generated 1,058 link clicks, 890 landing page views, 15 add-to-carts, $0.20 CPC, and 5.81% CTR on a premium-priced SKU.

A hot-audience bottom-of-funnel retargeting ad generated 11 add-to-carts at $3.25 cost per ATC with 5.47% CTR over five days. A middle-of-funnel retargeting ad generated 8 add-to-carts at roughly $7 cost per ATC, reinforcing that the segmented funnel structure was producing real behavioral movement.

Key Takeaways

Key Insight

Emerald Eyes could create desire before it had built institutional trust.

Attention, click-through, and initial buying intent were all present — the system was generating real add-to-cart behavior, and deeper-funnel signals showed users leaning in. The bottleneck was at the final threshold: trust, familiarity, authority. People were interested enough to explore, but not yet assured enough to commit.

That diagnosis shaped the next phase of the brand more than any single metric.

The gap was trust — and diagnosing that gap became more valuable than any single campaign metric.
